2.2 · Giving Claude a Role (Persona)

Role prompting for accuracy & tone

CONCEPT One sentence in system steers everything after it

Role prompting means putting a persona in the system prompt — "You are a senior tax attorney", "You are a terse SRE", "You are a patient primary-school teacher". Per the official docs: "Setting a role in the system prompt focuses Claude's behavior and tone for your use case. Even a single sentence makes a difference."

A role does two jobs at once:

  • Accuracy — it primes the right domain vocabulary, conventions, and depth. A "financial analyst" reads a balance sheet differently than a generic assistant.
  • Tone — it sets voice and register (formal vs. casual, terse vs. thorough) without you re-specifying it on every turn.
Role in system is sticky: because you resend system on every call, the persona persists across the whole conversation for free — you don't repeat "act as a lawyer" in each user message.
A role sets a perspective, not a fact-checker. "You are an expert" doesn't make Claude correct — it makes it answer as an expert would. Pair a role with real instructions, examples, and (for high-stakes work) verification. Don't treat a persona as a substitute for grounding.

CONCEPT Specific beats generic

The narrower and more concrete the role, the more leverage it has. "You are helpful" is nearly noise; "You are a helpful coding assistant specializing in Python" measurably changes output. Add the audience and goal when they matter: "…writing for non-technical executives; prioritize clarity over completeness."

CODE BAD → GOOD: same task, sharper role

The task is identical; only the system role changes. The GOOD role controls accuracy and tone in one line.

# BAD — no role: generic depth, unpredictable tone
client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-8", max_tokens=1024,
    messages=[{"role": "user",
               "content": "Review this SQL query for problems."}],
)

# GOOD — a specific persona focuses behavior AND voice
client.messages.create(
    model="claude-opus-4-8", max_tokens=1024,
    system=(
        "You are a senior database engineer reviewing production SQL. "
        "Flag correctness, performance, and injection risks. "
        "Be concise and cite the specific line for each issue."
    ),
    messages=[{"role": "user",
               "content": "Review this SQL query for problems:\n" + query}],
)

EXAM TRAPS

Trap: "Give Claude a role" means adding a role: "assistant" message. → No. Role prompting = writing a persona into the system parameter. The message role field (user/assistant) is a different concept.
Trap: Believing "You are an expert" makes answers factually correct. → A role sets perspective and tone, not truth. Ground high-stakes work with instructions, examples, and verification.
Trap: Vague roles ("be helpful"). → Specific roles (domain + audience + goal) do the steering; generic ones barely move output.
Trap: Re-declaring the persona in every user message. → Put it in system once; it's sticky and cacheable. Repeating it wastes tokens and can conflict with the task text.

PRACTICE

Q1 · What two things does a role in the system prompt control?

Accuracy (it primes the right domain knowledge, conventions, and depth) and tone (voice, register, verbosity). The docs note even a single sentence makes a difference.

Q2 · Why is a specific role better than "you are helpful"?

A specific role (domain + audience + goal) gives Claude a concrete perspective to reason from, which sharpens both correctness and voice. "Helpful" is too generic to steer anything.

Q3 · Does a role guarantee accuracy?

No — it sets how Claude answers, not whether the answer is true. It's a steering tool, not a grounding tool. Combine it with real instructions, examples, and verification for high-stakes tasks.
